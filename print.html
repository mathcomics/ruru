Ruru

Introduction

Ruru is a story about — well, you have to read it to find out.

The first two chapters were written in 2016, during an online writing course with Otis Haschemyer at Stanford University. Then the manuscript sat in my drive for ten years. Then I handed my plotline for the third chapter to Claude to complete. A few revisions later, here we are.

This book is a collaborative work. To contribute a chapter, see CONTRIBUTING.md.

Chapter 1. Death

The Flask

The sealed flask drifted away from the shore. If the flask had eyes of its own it would have seen Ruru’s large eyes following it from the beach. When it could not see her face any more, it would have seen her right hand shading her face and known that she was still trying to spot the flask floating away in the choppy waves. When it could not see her form any more it would have seen a speck of red in the backdrop of the stone walls of the fort of Lanka, and known that she was still standing there in her red checkered skirt. By dusk, when the torches at the guardhouses were lit, hundred paces apart, the southern wall of Lanka looked like a thin black jewel slowly sinking into the sea. By next morning the walls and towers were not visible any more. The three peaks of the Trikuta mountain were the only visible markers of where the flask had started its journey.

The Message

The message in the flask read - “My name is Ruru. I am nine. I live in a stone tower. The south gate of the city is across the yard from the tower. My father is the commander of the south gate. The city square used to be a short walk from here, cutting across Ashoka garden. But they closed the garden path when they brought the woman. Now we have to go all the way around the ramparts. There are a lot of guards in the garden now…”

The Flask

The flask floated aimlessly, occasionally pecked by a fish. On the second evening it was incessantly pelted by raindrops for a full quarter of an hour, the most exciting thing to have happened so far. Next midday, if it had looked up at the sky, it would have seen the flying form of a magnificent ape rushing through the clouds, towards the island of Lanka. His arms were stretched to the front as if parting the air. His tail swished lazily as if not able to figure out how it could help its owner in a flight, having spent all its life on ground, or in trees.

The Message

“I like the beach but sometimes it rains. Then I read. There are fourteen books in the tower. I have read all of them. Most of them are plans for walls and tunnels. Some are instructions or recipes. Two of them are stories of Shiva. I can write too. The other girls can’t. I write on the beach. I write on stones. My father gave me three palm leaves, dried and pressed, because I once wrote on a city plan and he was very mad. He also does not like me to wear his helm, or use his seal. I stole his seal again, so that I can stamp this flask when I am done.”

The Flask

For three nights the flask had floated in the dark sea. When the moon appeared occasionally it washed everything in white moonlight. But on the fourth night a reddish glow bounced of the flask’s polished surface, reflecting the bright red sky behind the Trikuta mountains. Red and black danced in the corner of the sky telling the story of fumes and frenzy that was not visible from this far. The red was gone by the morning, but clouds of black and grey huddled together around the mountain. If it were closer it could have seen the thick columns of smoke rising up from the city to feed the clouds. It could have smelled the stench and felt the warm ash descending like snowflakes. It would have seen the debris of a burnt city on the beach.

It tossed and turned. It tossed and turned again. The flask was stuck. The flask had not moved more than a yard in weeks. What are the odds that in the endless ocean it has hit a small skerry, and the rest of its days will be spent counting the motionless stars that watch it by the night, and the kelp that passes it by the day without pausing once.

The Message

“I have never been outside Lanka. My brother once went on a campaign. We watched him go on a boat. There were lots and lots of boats with other soldiers. Mother cried. She said he will not come back. But he came back. He brought me painted clay figures from a town called .. I don’t remember. One of the figures was a dancer. We have dancers here too but they dress differently. Another figure was an ascetic, with long white beard and wearing an animal skin. We don’t have any ascetics here. The healer who came to treat my mother when she got sick also had beard just like that. But he is not an ascetic. He made some drink for mother from the stuff in his satchel. Mother got well by morning.”

—

“Things my father always carried. His coin purse. His seal. His tobacco. His comb. His keys. His knife. He is very proud of his position. He said if there was ever a siege of the city, the south gate would stop it first. Or fall first. We never had a siege. We had a storm. After the storm, when everything was fixed, a special festival was declared. There were dancers in the street, some also danced with flames. We went to the city square in a big group from the south gate. We had a cart with us loaded with food and drinks for our group. Kira’s brother and two others drank too much. They had to be piled on the cart on our way home.”

The Flask

The bridge of floating rocks had brought the army within feet of the skerry. Dark hairy fingers yanked the flask out. Two large eyes started at it for a brief moment, before it slipped and splashed into the waves again. The monkey cursed the slime that had developed around the flask. As the flask drifted away, he hesitated for a moment. Then curiosity gave way to fear, and he turned around, with quick strokes started swimming back to his own safety. His compatriots cheered him from the line of rocks floating miraculously on the ocean surface. As the flask moved alone towards its newfound freedom, hundreds of thousands of monkeys, large and small, young and old, shouting and singing, armed with rocks and sticks, marched along the bridge of floating rocks, towards the island of Lanka.

The Message

“At first I wanted to be a builder. After the big storm, I watched the builders from the city fix the damages. The fort was fine, but a lot of houses were broken. I know some tricks to build. When it rains, little streams appear everywhere going down the hill. We made dams with stones. If you pull a clump of soil with the roots of grass it is very good for sealing dams. One stream was flowing into an ant hill. I made a small dam and saved it from being flooded. The ants must have been scared, those who never went outside, to see water from somewhere to come and drown your world.”

The Flask

The shore first appeared like a wispy green line. The coconut trees popped out of the indistinct green very very slowly, but then the sea floor rose abruptly and struck the flask with mild violence, then again, but milder, and milder, until the blows became mere brushings. The waves pushed it forward one moment, then pulled it back the next. Each time the shore cradled it deeper in its laps, and the waves found it more and more difficult to reach. Finally the flask came to a rest, for the second time since it left the shore of Lanka many months ago.

The Message

“I will see the world. Because I have now decided to be a traveler. One week after mother died, when I was sitting above the south gate watching the soldiers form lines, and march around the yard, that is when I decided.

Probably the anthill did not survive anyway. The storm got really bad. At night the sound of conch shells came from each house, one by one, so that we know they are safe. The second night father asked all to move into the great hall. It is not as big as the great hall of the city palace (I have heard) so it got very crowded. Old Sukarshan told stories. He has traveled a lot. But all his stories were of raids, and the people they killed. He says he was in a hundred campaigns but he lost no hand or leg. But the way he speaks, I think he lost something though. I don’t want to be that kind of a traveler.

Thank you for reading my letter. I do not know you. Perhaps, one day when I am a great traveler, you will hear about me, and bring me this letter.“

The Flask

“Don’t slip,” someone shouted. “What, are you my mother?” Vena retorted as he placed his good leg carefully on one stone and used his tail to draw the object closer. The flask had been waiting at the beach for two weeks.

Vena held it out like a trophy as he limped back hurriedly to catch up with his band. Thousands of monkeys were still arriving by the floating bridge, many of them with injuries, all of them weary and homesick after the thirteen day war. Vena had a flint in his left leg, which a healer had patched up. When he marched along with the returning army, the glitter of victory shone in his eyes. But the wound had festered, and he knew that he would not make it home.

Sula watched the scene from a distance. Sula had seen it first, the moment he had stepped on the shore, but his lack of curiosity had saved his life more than once, and he wasn’t going to change that for a piece of slimy trinket. In the battle of the bridge, he had stayed back, when his band brothers, curious to see the giant who sleeps six months, had rushed ahead. The giant’s menacing frame thundering above everything but the sky had sated their curiosity, and the giant’s foot crushed their skulls moments later.

Vena’s trophy passed around many hands before Sula held it close to his eyes, and squinted at the stamp. He chuckled loudly and reached into his waistband to pull out a seal — he had picked it from the rubble of the south tower, six days after the war had ended. The pattern on his seal matched the impression on the sealing wax on the flask.

“Where did you find that seal?”

“The south tower. This big brute who led the garrison gave us a real fight. He was still laughing when three stocky hairballs from Angad’s unit clubbed him down. His folks in the tower were caught in a fire. The entrance to the great hall had collapsed. We dug the bodies out from the rubble six days later, after the war had ended. A girl in a red checkered skirt had the seal clutched in her hand.”

As the flask changed hands, his voice trailed off but his thoughts continued. The war claims more than just soldiers. He was now thinking of his own home. His son was of a similar age that the girl had been, about nine. This was the longest he has been from home. Sula did not want to travel again. In this one campaign, he had seen enough for a lifetime. He did not lose an arm or leg, but he surely lost something.

(Not an) Epilogue

This is where this story would have ended, if Ruru had died.

But this story is not about war. This story is not about death. This story is about love.

Chapter 2. Resurrection

Cost of War.

Ruru remembered that there was a fire in the great hall, a loud noise, a sharp pain, and everything had gone dark.

Now the pain is gone. The din of war is gone. Only the sound of the sea. Ruru opened her eyes and saw a radiant face with three eyes smiling at her. Durga. The goddess of war, who battled the demons riding her lion. She was robed in bright red, her demeanor brighter than her robe. The lion walked very slowly behind the goddess, and stopped to sniff at a piece of charred masonry near her feet.

“Ruru, sweet child.” Durga extended her arms. “I am .. am I .. hurt?” “You’ve died.”

Ruru tried to make sense of it. Questions swarmed her mind - What? When? How? But then she already knew the answers. She asked the one she did not know.

“Why?”

“Because a war was waged. And the cost of war must be paid.” “Why me?

The goddess didn’t answer. She rested her palm on the lion’s neck. The lion’s eyes were amber and very still. It licked her ankles.

“Why me, it was not my war.”

“All wars are everyones.”

“I want to live. I need to live. I need to see… so much more.”

Durga looked at her intently for what looked like a very long time. The lion did not move. Ruru could swear the waves at the beach had stopped as well.

“tathA-astu,” she said. “Let it be so. You shall live then, Ruru.”

The lion lifted its head, as if startled by those words, and jumped to catch up. Durga had started walking away, but not stopped speaking.

“You shall live longer than anyone you know. or anyone you will.” She paused..

“…You shall live forever, Ruru. You will travel to many lands. You will see many, many more wars. Wars that will be waged for various causes. But no matter what the cause, wars must be paid for. You will survive every war. But every time… every time there is a war near you, it will claim the one you love most.”

Kurukshetra.

It was a very different war than the one long ago — many lifetimes ago, she had stopped counting — when she was a little girl nearly as old as her son was then. This one was a war to end all wars. That was how the old mahout had described it. Like there was such a thing. The war to end all wars was now over. Tonight there was relief written over all faces. Or maybe just drunken stupor. Wagons were being loaded. Tomorrow morning caravans would leave in all directions. During the war, Ruru had lingered close to the plains, waiting for the end. After the news came, she had arrived at the camp before sunlight had completely left the skies. She wanted to hear firsthand, before the caravans left, before the tales of this war were scattered across hundred kingdoms on dusty feet and creaky wheels.

Ruru wrapped her son closer. He had Mihir’s habit of tilting his head when he listened, as if the words might slide off otherwise. She listened silently as the old mahout spoke. He spoke of many things. He spoke of the first day of the war when he had arrived on his elephant, into a vast sea of men and animals, spears, and banners. He spoke of the last day when the few that were left of it tried to find a meaning of all this.

“…The elephant died on the seventh day. They gave me a spear and told me to join the ranks of spearmen.”

Like the sparks from the firewood burning before him, the mahout’s words lingered briefly in the air before disappearing into the night sky.

“Girl, these old eyes have seen many wars, but my first day on foot …God…I have seen the dust of battle, but down there I felt the dust prick my skin, I tasted it in my mouth. I have heard the sound of horse-hooves, but there I felt it, I felt it shaking the ground under my feet, and the waves rising up my spine. I never thought I would make it through one more day…”

But now the war was over. The mahout had survived. Now he got to tell the tale of those who did not.

He told Ruru how Mihir had died.

Ruru had thought about the war long before it was a certainty. It was always a certainty for her. She thought about it when she first saw Mihir. When Mihir slept, exhausted in love, Ruru stayed awake, running her fingers in his hair, and devising ways to cheat the war. She thought of ingenious schemes, some of which she had tried before, fruitlessly. She had seen many wars, across many centuries, and paid for each of them. And yet, when this war broke out, she did not try to stop Mihir. There was no doubt that he would go. There was no doubt that he would not return.

After the mahout stopped speaking, they listened to the song that made its way from the larger fire, and the even larger gathering further down the camp. The child had fallen asleep. Ruru got up. She placed him inside the tent and stepped outside. The camp had gotten much quieter, and many slept wherever they sat, not bothering to find their tent. Her feet stopped at a small makeshift shrine with a lonely lamp. A string of dry flowers. Three eyes etched on a stone in red ink.

A shrine for Durga.

Ruru grit her teeth, partly in resolve, partly in anger. She sat down, to let the anger drain, to let the resolve remain. Only after the lamp went out, she returned to her tent.

Night of War

Ruru woke up to find her son tugging her arm. The night still had a long way to go, but there was a commotion outside. “Ma, something going on, noises, the general’s tent..” He leaned outside. “No, STOP”. Ruru cried, suddenly alarmed. But he had already left the tent. There were all sorts of sounds outside, grunts or shrieks, animal or human, or a little bit of everything, Ruru couldn’t make sense as she hurriedly got up. She pushed herself out through the tent flap. She immediately fell back as two riderless black stallions galloped past her. The closest one passed within inches of her face. She could feel its hot breath.

She looked in the direction the horses came from, and saw a small thing lying on the ground twenty feet away. Every drop of pain in her body converged and lumped in her throat.

A large conflagration had lit up the eastern sky, and the fire was spreading rapidly, claiming tent after tent. The horses ran wild and tripped and kicked and cried in frenzy. Men who survived the rout made for the fence but arrows rained on them from the dark nothingness outside. The images and sounds went through Ruru without a sensation. Her mind was numb, her hands clutched the lifeless body of her son. Somewhere far away the elephants wailed. A tent pole snapped somewhere behind her. Fire reached the grain stores and the ground shook. Men called for each other in the dark. The calls stopped.

A small flame appeared behind the larger tent on her left, and within moments the entire tent was engulfed. Ruru ignored the heat on her face and the cries within the tent. Two men stepped out, then one fell to the ground with a short cry and the other followed without a whimper. She recognized the mahout as one of them. The man who had survived the elephant, the spear, the eighteen days of war, had not survived the night that followed. Then she saw the assailant emerge behind them, drawing his sword out of one victim. His hair was tied in a knot, and a sword in each of his hands looked like extensions of his arms. He effortlessly sliced through anything that came in his way. He looked like death. Ruru swiftly rose and stood in his way.

The assailant did not see her. He went past.

“Do you pity me? YOU… Do you think I am better alive? Do you know the curse of eternal life? You do not. I hope you did. I hope you will ..someday…beg to die …”

The assailant did not stop. He did not stop until the camp was quiet again. His name was Aswatthama. He had enough to pay for. The curse of eternal life, let that be one more.

Chapter 3. Love

Ruth

The house had a verandah that faced the garden, and in the cool of the early morning, before the heat arrived, Ruth would sit there with a book. More often than not, by the time she had read three pages, Rani would appear at the corner of the garden path with something in her hands — a cup, a cloth, an excuse — and they would talk until the heat drove them inside. Ruth’s mother had tried more than once to explain the correct distance to be maintained with the servants. Ruth’s father had given up trying.

Since they had moved to Kanpur, they had lived twenty yards apart — Ruth in the bungalow, Rani in the quarters on the other side of the low wall — and the city had quietly become the only one either of them had. Rani spoke better English than most of the clerks at the collector’s office, because she had learned it the way Ruth had learned to cook, which is to say badly and enthusiastically and over many years of mutual instruction.

Rani

When the news came it came in pieces, the way all bad news comes, each piece seeming to be the last, each piece followed by another. First the sepoys at Meerut. Then the regiments here. Then the magazine blown up in the night, the sound of it rolling across the city like thunder that would not stop.

Rani’s aunt, who had no authority over anyone, told her not to go. Rani went anyway. She did not ask herself why. She went because she had seen the way Ruth’s face went carefully blank when she was frightened. She did not want Ruth’s face to do that with no one there who would notice.

The entrenchment by the time she arrived was already full — British families crowded into the barracks building that offered almost no shade. She found Ruth near the east wall, sitting on the ground with her back against the mud bricks, her blue dress already the colour of the dust. Ruth looked up when Rani arrived and said nothing for a moment. Then she moved aside to make room.

They stayed close for the next three weeks. The temperature climbed. The water ran low. The guns fired from across the perimeter and the entrenchment grew smaller every day. Rani learned to count the hours by the pattern of the guns. She did not let herself think about what happened if the entrenchment fell. She thought instead about very small things: whether Ruth had drunk enough water that morning, whether the shade would shift by afternoon, whether the guns were closer or farther than yesterday.

Ruth

When the cease-fire came and they were told they would be given safe passage to Allahabad, Rani insisted on going with Ruth. Ruth wanted her there and was afraid of wanting it at the same time. She said nothing either way.

The morning they were to leave, Ruth kept her eyes on the road ahead. She was trying to think only about the next step and the one after that.

At the corner where the road narrowed before it turned towards the river, the crowd slowed. Ruth nearly tripped on the cat before she saw it — and then she saw the old woman, and the woman was already looking at her. Then Rani took her arm and pulled her forward.

“Come.”

The ghat was louder than Ruth had expected — hundreds of people, the creak and knock of the boats against each other, children crying, orders being called across the water. She was still trying to find which boat they had been told to board when the first shot was fired.

She did not know where it came from. The crowd reversed around her like a tide and then she understood that what she was smelling was smoke, and that the smoke was coming from the river. She could not see clearly. She could feel Rani’s hand on her arm and she held onto that.

They went with the crowd back up from the ghat, into the lanes above the river. Some ran. Some pressed into doorways and did not move. Ruth ran. She kept her hand on Rani’s arm and ran.

At the fork in the lane the crowd surged and drove between them. She reached back and found nothing.

The crowd surge had carried her down, back towards the river. She found herself at the base of the ghat steps, pressed against the stone wall, the weight of people above her making it impossible to move. The smoke was thicker here. She could hear horses on the road above and shouting and another sound she did not want to identify.

A soldier was coming down the steps through the crowd, moving against the current of people. He had a sword. He was not hurrying.

Rani

She had hold of Ruth’s arm when the first shot was fired. The crowd reversed. The boats were burning. She did not let go.

They ran with the crowd back up the ghat steps. At the top, where the steps met the road, a surge drove between them. Rani felt Ruth’s arm slip from her hand.

She turned. Ruth was not there.

For a moment she stood in the press of strangers and felt the particular cold of a fear she had been keeping at arm’s length for three weeks. She turned back. She called Ruth’s name. Her voice went into the noise and did not come back.

She pushed back against the crowd, back towards the river, back down the ghat steps — moving against everyone, not choosing to be quick, the way she had gone to the entrenchment without deciding to go, the way she had refused to leave without deciding to refuse. She did not think. She moved.

She saw Ruth before she saw the soldier. Ruth was pressed against the wall at the base of the steps, hemmed in by the crowd above her, not moving. The soldier was four steps above her, coming down with a sword at his side, unhurried.

Rani took the steps fast. She came between them. She felt Ruth’s hands seize her shoulders from behind — Ruth had turned and seen — and she heard Ruth say her name the way people say a name when it is too late.

The blade caught her below the right ribs. She felt it as a pressure first, then as heat. The soldier moved past them without stopping, on down the steps, already looking for the next thing.

Rani sat down on the steps. Ruth was beside her, Ruth’s voice saying her name in the particular way of a person trying to call someone back from somewhere they are already going.

“You didn’t have to,” Ruth said, crying.

Rani looked at her.

That was all there was time for.

Ruth

Ruth held her for a long time on the steps, after she had gone still.

When Ruth finally raised her head, the old woman was standing at the bottom of the steps. She had the cat at her feet.

Ruth looked at her.

“She shouldn’t have…,” Ruth said. Something had broken in the mechanism of her voice. “I wouldn’t have… died.”

“I know,” the old woman said.

“No.” Ruth’s voice sharpened. “You don’t. You haven’t seen…”

The old woman did not flinch. “I have.” She paused. “My child. I have watched you. Forever.”

The cat moved silently towards her. Its eyes were amber and very still. It licked her ankles.

“You,” Ruth said, as she recognized.

“You and I,” she said. The old woman’s face fell away, and what remained was not old and had a radiance that had no source Ruth could name.

—

“I am the glory of war. You are the cost of war.”

She paused.

“We are the same.”

“We are not the same,” Ruth said.

“No,” she said. “But we are inseparable.”

“You will not win. I will never love again.”

She looked at Ruth for a long time. Then she turned and walked away, the cat a step behind her, into the grey of the morning.

—

At dawn Ruru rose and walked out of the city. The river had taken back what the river always takes back, and the steps were quiet. She walked through the lanes above the river, past the old customs house, through the part of the city where nothing was burning anymore. She had walked out of a hundred cities that no longer existed. She had seen a hundred wars and paid her price for each of them.

I have told you before, this story is not about war. This story is not about death. This story is about love. On that cool morning, Ruru looked forward and started walking.

To love again.
